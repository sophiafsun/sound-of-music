What makes a song popular?

It's getting harder and harder for new artists to break into the music industry. In the age of endless streaming, we're exposed to more new music than ever before, at a faster pace than ever before. Meanwhile, we consider some of these bops to be classics while others are forgotten in history.

That raises the question, why do we remember and immortalize some songs for decades (we're looking at you Mariah Carey), while others never even make it into the Top 100*?

Is there a reason why a lot of mainstream music is starting to sound the same? Imagine there was formula for chart topping songs. How would you create the next hit single?

Let's find out.
* We are using the Billboards Top 100 chart as a measure of song popularity.

Let's take a look at the songs over the past six decades that have reached the #1 spot on the Billboard Top 100 chart. Songs are sized by the number of weeks they've been on the chart, then the 100 longest running have been selected for each category.

Clearly, each of these top songs must be doing something right.

    Valence is a measure of how positive, happy, cheerful, and euphoric a song is.

    Speechiness explains how much of a song is composed of spoken words.

    Liveness measures the probability that a song was performed live.

    Instrumentalness is a metric for whether or not a song includes vocals.

    Energy is a measurement of intensity, activity, and noisiness.

    Danceability is a combination of tempo, rhythm stability, beat strength, and regularity.

    Acousticness is a measurement of how likely the song does not involve electrical amplification.

    Okay, so now what?

    So that's it. We've seen the rise and fall of genres over the ages, but pop and rap consistently have topped the charts in recent years. But what does that really mean?

    Pop and rap songs have two things in common - they're high in both danceability and energy. Think about it: these days people want to dance, shimmy, and sing along to their favorite songs.

    Still want to be popstar? Make your next hit a danceable, high energy song, and it'll top the charts. We promise.

    Data Sources & Methods

    All of our source codes and datasets can be found in our Github repo.
    This website is a final project for CS171 at Harvard University in Fall 2020.
    Data Sources
    • Billboard Top 100 Weekly Charts: a compiled dataset of songs that have been popular over the past six decades. Used as the basis for all visualizations.
    • Spotify API: a compiled dataset of popular features of songs, used in tandem to our Billboard dataset to track audio trends. It is present in our last three visualizations.
    Methods
    • Due to there being a large variety of genres in our initial dataset we have decided to combine the them into a few "super-genres" for simplicity and analytical purposes.
    • Some songs within the dataset were assigned "miscellaneous" or "unclassified" as a genre. We have excluded these songs from our analysis.
